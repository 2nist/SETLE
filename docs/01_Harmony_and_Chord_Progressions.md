# Harmony & Chord Progressions
*Reference compiled from: "2-5-1 Progressions" & "Bass Theory" (John C. Goodman)*

---

## 1. Harmonic Functions (The Big Three)

Every chord in tonal music serves one of three functions:

| Function | Chords | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Tonic (T)** | I, vi | Stability — "home base" |
| **Pre-Dominant (PD)** | ii, IV | Movement away from tonic |
| **Dominant (D)** | V, vii° | Tension — strong pull back to I |

The classic cycle: **T → PD → D → T**
Example in C: `C - F - G - C` or `C - Dm - G - C`

## 2. Scale Degrees & Their Chord Roles

| Degree | Name | Chord in C Major | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Tonic | C Major | Home, resolution |
| ii | Supertonic | D Minor | Pre-dominant; leads to V |
| iii | Mediant | E Minor | Weak tonic substitute |
| IV | Subdominant | F Major | Pre-dominant; moves away from tonic |
| V | Dominant | G Major | Tension; strong resolution to I |
| vi | Submediant | A Minor | Relative minor; tonic substitute |
| vii° | Leading Tone | B Diminished | Strongest tension; always resolves to I |

### Scale of Chords (in C Major, with 7ths)
`Cmaj7 — Dm7 — Em7 — Fmaj7 — G7 — Am7 — Bm7♭5`

### Scale of Chords (in A Minor, with 7ths)
`Am7 — Bm7♭5 — Cmaj7 — Dm7 — Em7 — Fmaj7 — G7`

## 3. Common Chord Progressions

### Essential / Pop
| Name | Roman Numerals | C Major Example |
|---|---|---|
| Classic pop | I–V–vi–IV | C–G–Am–F |
| 50s progression | I–vi–IV–V | C–Am–F–G |
| Circle of fifths | vi–ii–V–I | Am–Dm–G–C |
| I–IV–V–I | I–IV–V–I | C–F–G–C |

### Jazz
| Name | Roman Numerals | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ii–V–I | ii–V–I | The backbone of jazz harmony |
| ii–V–I (tritone sub) | ii–♭II7–I | G7 → Db7 in C major |
| Backdoor | ii–♭VII7–I | Creates mystery/intrigue |
| Rhythm changes (A section) | I–vi–ii–V | Jazz standard foundation |
| Coltrane changes | I–V/♭VI–♭VI–V/III–III–V–I | Complex key-center cycling |

### Blues
| Name | Pattern |
|---|---|
| 12-bar blues | I–I–I–I–IV–IV–I–I–V–IV–I–V |

## 4. Cadences (How Phrases End)

| Cadence | Motion | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Perfect Authentic** | V → I | Strongest closure |
| **Imperfect Authentic** | V7 → I | Strong, slightly softer |
| **Plagal** | IV → I | "Amen" cadence, softer |
| **Deceptive** | V → vi | Unexpected; avoids resolution |
| **Half Cadence** | ? → V | Pause; leaves tension unresolved |
| **Tierce de Picardie** | i → I (minor → major) | Hopeful ending in minor key |

## 5. Harmonic Systems Overview

| System | Definition | Key Features | Common Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Diatonic** | Stays within one key | No chromaticism | Pop, folk, rock |
| **Functional** | Chords assigned T/PD/D roles | Tension-resolution cycle | Classical, jazz |
| **Modal** | Based on modes, avoids V–I | Atmospheric, less "pull" | Jazz, experimental rock |
| **Tonal** | Western classical system | Major-minor, voice leading | Classical, orchestral |

## 6. Modes — Quick Reference

| Mode | Degrees | Formula | Character | Chord Quality |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ionian** (I) | 1-2-3-4-5-6-7 | W-W-H-W-W-W-H | Bright, happy | Maj7 |
| **Dorian** (II) | 1-2-♭3-4-5-6-♭7 | W-H-W-W-W-H-W | Minor+raised 6th, jazzy | m7 |
| **Phrygian** (III) | 1-♭2-♭3-4-5-♭6-♭7 | H-W-W-W-H-W-W | Dark, Spanish, exotic | m7 |
| **Lydian** (IV) | 1-2-3-♯4-5-6-7 | W-W-W-H-W-W-H | Airy, dreamy | maj7 |
| **Mixolydian** (V) | 1-2-3-4-5-6-♭7 | W-W-H-W-W-H-W | Bluesy, dominant | 7 (dominant) |
| **Aeolian** (VI) | 1-2-♭3-4-5-♭6-♭7 | W-H-W-W-H-W-W | Sad, dark | m7 |
| **Locrian** (VII) | 1-♭2-♭3-4-♭5-♭6-♭7 | H-W-W-H-W-W-W | Unstable, dissonant | m7♭5 |

**Key insight:** If you're playing over a G7 chord → use Mixolydian. Minor chords → Dorian or Aeolian depending on whether the ♭6 or natural 6 is referenced.

## 7. Chord Substitutions

### Smooth / Diatonic
| Original | Sub | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| I (C) | vi (Am) | Softer, voice-led |
| IV (F) | ii (Dm) | Jazzier, smoother motion |
| V (G) | iii (Em) | Weak, introspective |

### Dominant Substitutions
| Original | Sub | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| V7 (G7) | ♭II7 / Tritone (Db7) | Jazz tension, chromatic motion |
| V (G) | ♭VII (Bb) | Bluesy, Mixolydian feel |
| V (G) | V/V (D7) | Secondary dominant; stronger pull |

### Modal / Borrowed Chords
| Original | Sub | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| IV (F) | iv (Fm) | Melancholic, jazz color |
| I (C) | ♭III (Eb) | Bluesy, dramatic |
| V (G) | ♭VII (Bb) | Rock/folk, removes leading tone |

### Chromatic
| Original | Sub | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| I→ii (C→Dm) | I→I#dim→ii (C→C#dim→Dm) | Smooth chromatic connection |
| ii (Dm) | ♭II / Neapolitan (Db) | Classical drama, film tension |

## 8. Harmonic Transitions Between Song Sections

### Verse → Chorus
- **Half cadence (V):** builds anticipation → chorus arrives on I
- **Secondary dominant (V/V→V→I):** extra momentum
- **Deceptive cadence (V→vi):** surprise; delays full chorus resolution

### Chorus → Bridge
- **Move to vi or IV:** creates contrast
- **Modal shift:** e.g., major chorus → minor bridge for emotional depth

### Bridge → Final Chorus
- **Modulate up half/whole step:** climactic lift (classic pop device — see: Whitney Houston)
- **Sustain V (dominant pedal):** tension-filled lead-in

### Outro
- **Plagal cadence IV→I:** gospel "amen" close
- **Fade on looping vamp:** dreamlike, open-ended
- **Tierce de Picardie:** minor song ends on major I for hopeful resolution

## 9. Secondary Dominants

A **secondary dominant** is a dominant chord applied to a chord other than I, temporarily making it sound like a "local tonic."

Written as **V/x** (dominant of another degree).

| Secondary Dom | In C Major | Resolves To |
|---|---|---|
| V/ii | A7 | Dm |
| V/iii | B7 | Em |
| V/IV | C7 | F |
| V/V | D7 | G |
| V/vi | E7 | Am |

Example: `C – E7 – Am – D7 – G – C` (I – V/vi – vi – V/V – V – I)

## 10. Chromaticism & Special Chords

**Tritone Substitution:** Replace V7 with the dominant chord a tritone away. Both share the same tritone interval (3-7 / 7-3 swap).
- G7 → Db7 in C major. The Db7 resolves to C by half-step motion.

**Augmented 6th Chords:** Pull strongly to V.
- Italian: (♭6, 1, ♯4)
- French: (♭6, 1, 2, ♯4)
- German: (♭6, 1, ♭3, ♯4)

**Neapolitan Chord (♭II):** Major chord on the lowered 2nd degree. Usually in first inversion. Dramatic pre-dominant.
- In C: `Db – G – C`

**Chromatic Mediants:** Altered iii or vi for color.
- C → E major (instead of Em) → F → G
