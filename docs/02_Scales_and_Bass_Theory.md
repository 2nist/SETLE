# Scales & Bass Theory
*Reference compiled from: "Bass Theory" by John C. Goodman*

---

## 1. The Fundamentals

**Notes** are named pitches: A–B–C–D–E–F–G (then repeat). The distance from A to A (one repeat) = one **Octave**.

A single letter can refer to:
- A note (pitch)
- A chord (e.g., C major)
- A key (key of C)
- A scale (C major scale)
- A root or tonic

**Tones & Semitones:**
- 1 **whole tone** = 2 semitones = 1 whole step
- 1 **semitone** = half step = one fret on bass
- Sharps (♯) raise a note by a semitone; flats (♭) lower by a semitone

---

## 2. The Major Scale

**Formula:** W–W–H–W–W–W–H (W = whole step, H = half step)

In C: `C – D – E – F – G – A – B – C`

**Scale Degrees:**
| Degree | Name | Role |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Tonic | Home |
| 2 | Supertonic | — |
| 3 | Mediant | — |
| 4 | Subdominant | Pre-dominant |
| 5 | Dominant | Tension |
| 6 | Submediant | — |
| 7 | Leading Tone | Pulls to tonic |

---

## 3. The Natural Minor Scale

**Formula:** W–H–W–W–H–W–W

In A: `A – B – C – D – E – F – G – A`

Degrees: `1 – 2 – ♭3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6 – ♭7`

The **relative minor** starts on the 6th degree of its relative major. Key of C major → A natural minor (same notes, different root).

---

## 4. Alternate Minor Scales

### Harmonic Minor
Raises the ♭7 to a natural 7 (the leading tone). Creates a strong pull back to tonic.
- In Am: `A – B – C – D – E – F – G♯ – A`
- Degrees: `1 – 2 – ♭3 – 4 – 5 – ♭6 – 7`
- Step pattern: W–H–W–W–H–WH–H (note the 1.5 step gap between ♭6 and 7)
- **Use for:** Minor chord with major 7th (e.g., CmM7); smooth melodic resolution

### Melodic Minor (Jazz Minor)
Ascending: natural 6 and 7 (like major with ♭3). Descending: reverts to natural minor.
- Ascending Am: `A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G♯ – A`
- Degrees (ascending): `1 – 2 – ♭3 – 4 – 5 – 6 – 7`
- **Use for:** Avoiding the awkward 1.5-step interval in harmonic minor; jazz contexts

---

## 5. Pentatonic Scales

### Major Pentatonic
5 notes: degrees **1–2–3–5–6** of the major scale (drops 4 and 7).
- In C: `C – D – E – G – A`
- Bright, versatile; works over major progressions

### Minor Pentatonic
5 notes: degrees **1–♭3–4–5–♭7** of the natural minor scale.
- In Am: `A – C – D – E – G`
- Widely used in blues, rock; can be played over dominant 7 chords
- **Tip:** Minor pentatonic over major chords gives instant blues/rock character

---

## 6. The Blues Scale

The minor pentatonic + a ♭5 (the "blue note").
Degrees: **1–♭3–4–♭5–5–♭7**
- In A: `A – C – D – E♭ – E – G`
- The ♭5 creates tension that resolves to the natural 5
- Despite the minor 3rd, not classified as a minor scale — it's its own thing

---

## 7. The Dominant Scale (Mixolydian)

Starting on the 5th degree of a major scale gives the **Dominant scale**, identical to Mixolydian mode.

Formula: **W–W–H–W–W–H–W**
Degrees: `1 – 2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – 6 – ♭7`

- In C dominant (from F major): `C – D – E – F – G – A – B♭ – C`
- The only difference from C major: **B♭ instead of B**
- **Essential for:** Dominant 7th chords; Mixolydian playing over V7 chords

---

## 8. Intervals

Intervals describe the distance between two notes, measured in semitones.

| Semitones | Interval Name | Quality |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Minor 2nd | Dissonant |
| 2 | Major 2nd | — |
| 3 | Minor 3rd | Minor color |
| 4 | Major 3rd | Major color |
| 5 | Perfect 4th | Consonant |
| 6 | Tritone (Aug 4th / Dim 5th) | Most dissonant |
| 7 | Perfect 5th | Strong consonance |
| 8 | Minor 6th | — |
| 9 | Major 6th | — |
| 10 | Minor 7th | Tension |
| 11 | Major 7th | Strong tension → resolution |
| 12 | Octave | Perfect consonance |

**Tritone** (6 semitones) is the interval that drives dominant chord resolution. G7 contains B–F (a tritone), which resolves to C–E in C major.

## 9. Chords & Chord Construction

Chords are built by **stacking thirds** (every other scale degree).

### Triads
| Type | Formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Major | 1–3–5 | C–E–G |
| Minor | 1–♭3–5 | A–C–E |
| Diminished | 1–♭3–♭5 | B–D–F |
| Augmented | 1–3–♯5 | C–E–G♯ |

### 7th Chords
| Type | Formula | Example | Symbol |
|---|---|---|---|
| Major 7th | 1–3–5–7 | C–E–G–B | Cmaj7 |
| Minor 7th | 1–♭3–5–♭7 | A–C–E–G | Am7 |
| Dominant 7th | 1–3–5–♭7 | G–B–D–F | G7 |
| Minor Major 7th | 1–♭3–5–7 | — | CmM7 |
| Half-Diminished | 1–♭3–♭5–♭7 | B–D–F–A | Bm7♭5 |
| Diminished 7th | 1–♭3–♭5–♭♭7 | — | dim7 |

### Extensions (9th, 11th, 13th)
Add color and sophistication by stacking thirds beyond the 7th.
- **9th** = 2nd an octave up
- **11th** = 4th an octave up
- **13th** = 6th an octave up

### Other Chords
- **Power chord:** 1–5 (no 3rd — ambiguous major/minor; heavy rock)
- **Suspended (sus2/sus4):** Replace 3rd with 2nd or 4th — neither major nor minor
- **Slash chords:** Specific bass note under chord (e.g., G/B = G chord, B in bass)

## 10. Chord Inversions

| Inversion | Bass Note | Symbol |
|---|---|---|
| Root position | Root (1) | C |
| 1st inversion | 3rd | C/E |
| 2nd inversion | 5th | C/G |
| 3rd inversion (7th chords) | 7th | Cmaj7/B |

## 11. Bass-Specific Notes

**Standard tuning:** E–A–D–G (low to high)

Bass notation sounds one octave lower than written. Middle C (C4 on piano) = C3 on bass staff, played as C2 on the instrument.

**Fretboard principle:** Every fret = one semitone. Same note can appear in multiple positions — choose based on context, technique, and tone.

**Timbre:** Plucking near the bridge = bright/punchy; near the neck = warm/round. Position choices affect tone as much as notes.

---

## 12. The Circle of Fifths

Moving clockwise = ascending by perfect fifths (or descending by fourths). Each key adds one sharp (clockwise) or one flat (counterclockwise).

**Key relationships:**
- Keys adjacent on the circle are closely related (share most notes)
- Dominant chord of any key = the next key clockwise
- This is why ii–V–I works: Dm → G → C moves around the circle

**Practical use:** If a song keeps cycling through keys, it's likely moving in fourths or fifths. Coltrane changes are a sophisticated navigation of this principle.
